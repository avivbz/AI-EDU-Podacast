### 1. Feminist and Global South perspectives on AI-supported learning environments

1. **Title:** Feminist and Global South perspectives on AI-supported learning environments
2. **Publication date:** 9 July 2026
3. **Institution / publisher / platform:** The Brookings Institution (Center for Universal Education; "Education Plus Development" blog)
4. **Author(s):** Bhawana Shrestha, Anthony Luvanda, and Jamila Razzaq
5. **Author note:** The three authors are Echidna Global Scholars affiliated with Brookings who work on gender equality and education in the Global South; Shrestha co-founded My Emotions Matter, Luvanda is a senior IT lecturer at the National Defence University–Kenya and founder of Mabinti Tech CBO, and Razzaq co-founded the Aappa Aziz Trust.
6. **URL:** https://www.brookings.edu/articles/feminist-and-global-south-perspectives-on-ai-supported-learning-environments/
7. **Summary:**
The commentary examines artificial intelligence in education (AIEd) through a feminist and Global South lens, arguing that AIEd is frequently promoted as a solution to persistent problems such as teacher shortages, large class sizes, and uneven education quality, but that in its current design and deployment it risks reproducing existing gendered, economic, linguistic, and geopolitical inequalities. The authors distinguish learner-facing tools (adaptive learning platforms and tutoring systems), teacher-facing tools (for assessment, feedback, and administrative tasks), and institution-facing systems (analyzing enrollment, retention, and student progress), and contend that each carries embedded assumptions that can marginalize girls, rural learners, low-income students, and speakers of non-dominant languages.

The piece maps four "structural tensions": access versus meaningful participation (citing that only 27% of individuals in low-income countries are connected to the internet, and that in low-income countries about 10% of girls and young women aged 15–24 are online compared with 22% of boys and young men); contextual relevance versus epistemic injustice (systems designed in the Global North and exported without local-language or local-knowledge support); gender inclusion versus bias amplification (women underrepresented as users, designers, and decision-makers); and adoption versus constrained readiness (limited infrastructure, digital-literacy gaps, and governance concerns). It then offers policy and practice implications directed at governments, AI developers, and funders, including bridging infrastructure and inclusivity gaps, investing in local-language datasets and governance, redesigning systems with and for women and girls, and conditioning funding on equity standards.
8. **Main conclusions / central claims:** The authors argue that the barriers facing Global South learners are structural, gendered, and epistemic, and that addressing them requires governments and policymakers to invest in local and equitable infrastructure, knowledge, and governance. Their central claim is that for AI in education to work for all learners, the Global South must be positioned as a partner in the design and governance of these tools rather than only as a recipient of technologies developed elsewhere.

### 2. Introducing Claude for Teachers

1. **Title:** Introducing Claude for Teachers
2. **Publication date:** 14 July 2026
3. **Institution / publisher / platform:** Anthropic (company product announcement)
4. **Author(s):** Anthropic (corporate announcement; the company's education lead, Drew Bent, is quoted in related coverage)
5. **Author note:** Anthropic is an AI research and product company; this is its official announcement of a K–12 teacher-facing product, released as part of the company's stated education and "Beneficial Deployments" efforts.
6. **URL:** https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-for-teachers
7. **Summary:**
The announcement introduces "Claude for Teachers," a free product for verified K–12 educators in the United States that provides access to premium Claude capabilities, a library of teaching skills, and a connection to curricula mapped to academic standards in all 50 states. Anthropic states that the tool connects to "Learning Commons" for state standards and underlying learning progressions and to curricular resources including OpenSciEd and Illustrative Mathematics, and that it integrates with an ecosystem of K–12 tools such as ASSISTments, Brisk Teaching, Canva Education, Coteach, Diffit, Eedi, MagicSchool, Snorkl, and TeachFX. Described use cases include drafting standards-aligned lesson plans, differentiating materials for students at different readiness levels, analyzing folders of class data (rosters, diagnostics, attendance, teacher notes) to inform instruction, and scheduling repeated tasks such as reviewing daily exit tickets.

The post frames the product around research on differentiation, mastery-based learning, and small-group instruction, and cites Stanford's evidence review in stating that AI tools for teachers can strengthen instructional practice while the impact of student-facing tools is mixed. On privacy, Anthropic states that Claude for Teachers data is not used for model training and that student information is covered by a K–12 Data Processing Addendum written to comply with FERPA; it notes work with the American Federation of Teachers on a "gold standard" for safety and privacy, and quotes AFT President Randi Weingarten. The announcement also references a course, "AI Fluency for PK-12 Teachers" (co-created with Teach For America) and a train-the-trainer module with the AFT, an open-source repository of the teaching skills, and a planned pilot evaluation in the Detroit Public Schools Community District. The product is limited to educators (18-and-over policy), with a dedicated school/district offering described as forthcoming.
8. **Main conclusions / central claims:** The stated aim is to "close the gap between educational best practices and what a teacher's week allows" by supporting planning, differentiation, and data-informed instruction, while keeping teachers rather than students as the primary users and protecting student data from model training. Anthropic positions the release as part of a broader education effort, including released public goods (an open-source skills repository and a technical write-up) and a district pilot intended to study effects on educator well-being and practice.

### 3. Anthropic unveils Claude for Teachers, joining OpenAI and Google in race to dominate classroom AI

1. **Title:** Anthropic unveils Claude for Teachers, joining OpenAI and Google in race to dominate classroom AI (also headlined "Another contender just joined the arms race over AI in schools")
2. **Publication date:** 14 July 2026
3. **Institution / publisher / platform:** Chalkbeat (nonprofit education newsroom)
4. **Author(s):** Lily Altavena
5. **Author note:** Altavena is a national reporter at Chalkbeat who covers education and technology.
6. **URL:** https://www.chalkbeat.org/2026/07/14/anthropic-launches-claude-for-teachers-as-ai-companies-battle-for-classrooms/
7. **Summary:**
The article reports on Anthropic's launch of Claude for Teachers, describing it as an entry into a competition among technology companies — alongside Google, OpenAI, and Khan Academy — to market AI products to K–12 educators. It notes that the product can incorporate academic standards from all 50 states, can be used to devise lesson plans, personalize instructional materials, and harness data to improve instruction, and is free for verified U.S. educators, with a pilot planned in the Detroit Public Schools Community District to study educator well-being and practice. Anthropic's education lead, Drew Bent, is quoted describing how teachers could pull in a student's past assessment and assignment data and ask Claude to build individualized lesson plans, and noting that Detroit had already been using Claude products in what the company viewed as a "human-centric" way.

The piece situates the launch within what it calls a "complicated moment" for classroom technology: it notes that the American Federation of Teachers, the Trump administration, and Bill Gates have encouraged AI adoption, while a "parent-led uprising" against screen time is prompting some large districts to reconsider ed-tech contracts. It reports on privacy provisions (conversations in teacher accounts not used for training, student-privacy protections built to comply with federal law, and plain-language terms), Anthropic's work with the AFT on privacy standards, and Weingarten's quoted support — while noting she has called for a ban on student-facing AI in elementary grades. The article cites skeptics, including AEI research fellow Daniel Buck on "offloading" teacher work, references student-facing concerns about cheating and cognitive offloading, notes Utah's statewide deal to bring Google's Gemini to K–12 schools, and reports that roughly 61% of teachers surveyed by Education Week in 2025 said they used AI in some capacity, up from 32% in 2024.
8. **Main conclusions / central claims:** The article's central framing is that Anthropic's launch intensifies a competition among major technology companies to become the dominant classroom AI, and that this arrives amid unresolved tensions over screen time, student privacy, cheating, and cognitive offloading. It reports, without endorsing a position, that adoption of AI among teachers is rising even as skeptics and some district and union stances push in the opposite direction, and that it remains unclear whether any single AI product will prevail in schools.

### 4. July 14 Update: We MUST Act Now (Leaders); Human-Level AI gets closer; new OpenAI and xAI Models; Societal Disruption; Experiential Learning

1. **Title:** July 14 Update: We MUST Act Now (Leaders); Human-Level AI gets closer; new OpenAI and xAI Models; Societal Disruption; Experiential Learning
2. **Publication date:** 14 July 2026
3. **Institution / publisher / platform:** Education Disrupted: Teaching and Learning in An AI World (Substack)
4. **Author(s):** Stefan Bauschard
5. **Author note:** Bauschard is an education author and consultant focused on the implications of AI for teaching, learning, and assessment, and writes the "Education Disrupted" newsletter for educators and school leaders.
6. **URL:** https://stefanbauschard.substack.com/p/july-14-update-we-must-act-now-leaders
7. **Summary:**
The post is a periodic briefing addressed to education leaders "who have to open school in September." It opens by pointing to recent AI developments — including a newer model the author describes as able to orient itself in unfamiliar situations, and an open letter that the post says was signed by fifteen Nobel laureates and leading figures in AI and economics urging preparation for a rapidly arriving AI economy — and argues that the relevant question for schools has shifted from whether major economic change is coming to what it means to prepare a student to flourish in the emerging world.

The central argument is organized around a contrast between "book learning" and "experiential learning." Bauschard frames text-only or "language-only" learning as producing an understanding of the world "secondhand," analogous to how a language model learns from descriptions, while experiential learning — in which a student builds, argues, performs, fails, and recovers — is likened to the "act-predict-update loop" that AI labs are trying to give machines through "world models." From this analogy the post advances the proposition that, if machines require world models to reach general intelligence, then children require experiential learning to reach "true human intelligence," and it closes with a proposal for how school leaders might respond.
8. **Main conclusions / central claims:** The post's central claim is that schools should prioritize experiential, world-model-building learning over primarily text-based instruction as a response to advancing AI, and that education leaders should act during what the author calls the current window before more capable AI systems arrive. It presents this as an urgent, leader-directed call to redesign learning experiences rather than to make incremental adjustments.
