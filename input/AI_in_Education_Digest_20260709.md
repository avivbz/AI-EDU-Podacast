## Included publications (publication date confirmed within window)

### 1. Beyond access: Why AI literacy will define the future

1. **Title:** Beyond access: Why AI literacy will define the future
2. **Publication date:** 1 July 2026
3. **Institution / publisher / platform:** World Economic Forum (Forum Stories / Centre for the New Economy and Society)
4. **Author(s):** TeachAI Steering Committee
5. **Author note:** TeachAI is a global coalition of education leaders, policymakers, researchers, civil-society organizations, and technology companies (convened with partners including Code.org, ETS, and ISTE) that develops shared guidance on AI in schools; the piece also cites ISTE+ASCD and ETS leaders.
6. **URL:** https://www.weforum.org/stories/2026/07/beyond-access-why-ai-literacy-will-define-the-future/
7. **Summary:**
The article argues that the historic "digital divide" of access to devices and connectivity is being superseded by a new divide between people who can question, evaluate, and direct AI systems and those who only consume their outputs. It presents the release of the AI Literacy (AILit) Framework, a joint initiative of the European Commission and the OECD developed with input from more than 2,000 stakeholders across 100+ countries, as a milestone that offers a shared definition of the knowledge, skills, and attitudes learners need. The authors distinguish AI literacy from mere tool use, framing it as encompassing technical understanding, critical evaluation of outputs, recognition of limitations and bias, ethical reasoning, and the exercise of human judgment.

The article marshals survey data to establish the urgency of the issue: a 2025 survey of European teenagers found 88% of those aged 13–15 and 96% of those aged 16–18 use AI tools for learning and creative tasks at least several times a week, and a Common Sense Media study reported that 72% of surveyed US teenagers had used AI companions. It contrasts this rapid adoption with teacher readiness, citing the OECD's 2024 TALIS finding that only about one in three teachers uses AI and three in four report lacking the skills to teach with it. The piece closes by framing implementation—embedding AI literacy in curricula, teacher preparation, and assessment—as the critical next step.
8. **Main conclusions / central claims:** The authors contend that the decisive future gap will be one of understanding rather than access, that a shared framework such as AILit is necessary to prevent fragmented national approaches, and that defining AI literacy is only a first step whose value depends on embedding it in classrooms, teacher training, and assessment. They maintain that as AI grows more capable, distinctly human capacities—curiosity, judgment, creativity, empathy, ethical reasoning—become more, not less, important, and that action is required from education leaders, policymakers, employers, technology companies, and civil society.

### 2. The Latest Findings in AI and Learning – July 2026

1. **Title:** The Latest Findings in AI and Learning – July 2026
2. **Publication date:** 6 July 2026
3. **Institution / publisher / platform:** Filament Games (Game-Based Learning Review blog / Content Hub)
4. **Author(s):** Brandon Pittser
5. **Author note:** Pittser is a content and marketing lead at Filament Games, an educational game-development studio that publishes a recurring monthly roundup of research and news at the intersection of AI and edtech.
6. **URL:** https://www.filamentgames.com/blog/the-latest-findings-in-ai-and-learning---july-2026
7. **Summary:**
This monthly roundup curates and summarizes five recent items on AI in education, organized around the recurring theme of "keeping humans in the loop." It reports on a UNESCO regional conference in Sarajevo that launched AI-in-education resources in local languages and emphasized safeguarding educational integrity and critical thinking; a World Economic Forum piece warning that students may treat AI outputs as unquestioned authority given the Western, English-dominant training data behind most large language models; and Microsoft's 2026 AI in Education report, which found that 92% of students and education leaders had used AI for school while roughly 77% of students reported no formal training. Each item is paired with the author's framing of implications for game-based and simulation-based learning.

The roundup also summarizes a GIS Reports analysis arguing that automated tools risk supplanting the intellectual effort needed for genuine comprehension and advocating institutional diversity over centralized planning, and an NPR/Ipsos poll of K–12 teachers reporting that 60% use AI for work tasks while 54% believe it makes it harder for students to learn critical thinking and 59% feel it is eroding student–teacher trust. Throughout, the post connects these findings to a case for "verifiable, play-based assessment" as a way to preserve cognitive development. (Several of the underlying primary sources it cites—e.g., the UNESCO Sarajevo article, the Microsoft report, and the NPR/Ipsos poll—carry publication dates outside this digest's 10-day window; they are noted here only as items summarized within the roundup.)
8. **Main conclusions / central claims:** The post's central claim is that while AI offers personalization, keeping humans in the loop through active, verifiable assessment is essential to preserving cognitive development, and that educational game design is well positioned to deliver transparent tools that foster critical thinking without compromising trust. It presents the aggregated evidence as showing simultaneously high AI adoption and high anxiety among educators, and frames a demand for structured, guided, guardrailed integration.

### 3. Analog Is Having a Moment. But Is It Sustainable?

1. **Title:** Analog Is Having a Moment. But Is It Sustainable?
2. **Publication date:** 8 July 2026
3. **Institution / publisher / platform:** Rhetorica (Substack)
4. **Author(s):** Marc Watkins
5. **Author note:** Watkins is Assistant Director of Academic Innovation, Director of the Mississippi AI Institute, and a lecturer in writing and rhetoric at the University of Mississippi who trains faculty in AI literacy and is a co-author of *The Norton Guide to AI-Aware Teaching*.
6. **URL:** https://marcwatkins.substack.com/p/analog-is-having-a-moment-but-is
7. **Summary:**
The post examines the growing interest in "analog," low-tech, and distraction-reduced learning as a response to AI-saturated classrooms. Prompted by a proposal for campus "Human Intelligence Labs," Watkins weighs the appeal of deeply personal, offline learning against the practical constraints faced by overworked faculty and diverse student populations. He enumerates design challenges—risks of privileging in-person residential courses over online and large-lecture formats, changed student attendance patterns, accommodation needs for students with disabilities who rely on digital devices, the need for out-of-class access to drafts in writing-intensive courses, and the high labor and space costs of building reduced-distraction environments.

Watkins argues that an analog approach should function as one part of a broader "AI-aware" teaching ecology rather than a standalone solution, proposing a mix of "focus days" in low-distraction spaces and "open days" where digital tools are available with guidance. He profiles several faculty practices (Mark Marino's "analog sandwich," James Seitz's in-class "focus mode" writing, and structured analog workbooks), and contends that most course-level AI rules are effectively faculty preferences rather than coherent policy. He frames the ultimate goal as helping students develop "discernment"—knowing when to use AI and when to step back—which he says requires structured opportunities to compare AI-mediated and non-AI learning.
8. **Main conclusions / central claims:** Watkins concludes that the sustainability of analog learning depends on treating it as a serious design challenge integrated into AI-aware teaching rather than as backlash or a policing mechanism; that if analog learning becomes merely a way to surveil student AI use, or a privilege available only in certain courses or campuses, it will fail. He claims that the fragmented, preference-driven policy environment does not teach discernment, and that the central aim of teaching in the AI era should be cultivating students' own judgment and values about when to use AI.

### 4. "Humanity Amplified" serialization (Chapters 5–14)

1. **Title:** *Humanity Amplified* book serialization, including "Losing Students: Existential Challenges for Schools Built for a Different World (Chapter 5)" and subsequent chapters through "Conclusion and a Moon Shot (Chapter 14)"
2. **Publication date:** 5 July 2026 – 9 July 2026 (multiple posts; Chapter 5 dated 5 July 2026)
3. **Institution / publisher / platform:** Education Disrupted: Teaching and Learning in An AI World (Substack)
4. **Author(s):** Stefan Bauschard
5. **Author note:** Bauschard is an education author and consultant focused on the implications of AI for teaching, learning, and assessment, and writes the "Education Disrupted" newsletter for educators and school leaders.
6. **URL:** https://stefanbauschard.substack.com/p/losing-students-existential-challenges (Chapter 5); series archive: https://stefanbauschard.substack.com/archive
7. **Summary:**
Across a rapid sequence of posts during the window, Bauschard serialized chapters of his book *Humanity Amplified*, several of which address AI and schooling directly. The anchor chapter, "Losing Students" (Chapter 5), argues that schools are treating AI as one more classroom technology to be managed when they are in fact facing an economic transition comparable to the one that produced mass schooling. He contends that industrial-era schooling rested on five assumptions—that knowledge is scarce, that instruction must be batched, that learning is front-loaded, that the product is standardized cognition, and that assessment must rely on proxies such as seat time—and that generative AI has falsified all five in the same direction, making instruction abundant and "formation" scarce.

Later chapters in the series (Chapters 6–14) shift toward proposed responses, emphasizing "academic deep learning" and human development, and profiling approaches such as project-based learning, robotics, entrepreneurship education, debate, design thinking, and maker spaces, before concluding with a chapter framing the AI moment as analogous to the post-Sputnik "moon shot." The posts draw on figures including Bloom's "two-sigma" tutoring research, the Carnegie unit's history, and reported declines in the cost of running AI models. (Some individual posts are partially paywalled; summaries here reflect the publicly available portions and previews.)
8. **Main conclusions / central claims:** Bauschard's central claim is that the current AI transition calls for redesigning schooling rather than patching it, and that restriction-based responses (such as phone bans) will not reverse student disengagement or stop learning from migrating to tutors, videos, and models outside the school. He argues that the institution's remaining monopoly—verification of what students can do—is beginning to crack, warns of "stratified secession" in which wealthier families exit to AI-native learning while others are left behind, and proposes that schools re-found themselves around what he calls deep learning: judgment, purpose, attention, and formation.
